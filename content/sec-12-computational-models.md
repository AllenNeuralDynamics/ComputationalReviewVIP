# Computational Models of VIP Function

## 12.0 Framing

{ref}`sec-species-translation` framed the cross-species caveat: the mouse-derived VIP cartoon is a scaffold, not a blueprint, for human cortex. The question for the present section is whether the formal models of VIP circuit function inherit the same mouse-centric assumptions. Sections {ref}`sec-circuit-motifs` through {ref}`sec-oscillations` assembled the empirical case against a tidy, universal VIP→SST→PYR disinhibitory motif: wiring is heterogeneous across cortical areas and subtypes, behavioral correlates dissociate from disinhibition in several tasks, and gamma/theta coupling results diverge across studies. Computational models are where those empirical dissociations either survive formalization or quietly vanish into parameter-tuning. This section asks a narrower question than the canonical motif debate itself: **when a model is written down with equations, which VIP-circuit phenomena actually require disinhibition, and which only require a four-population architecture with the right gain terms?**

Two organizing claims structure the section. First, the architectural space of published VIP-circuit models is smaller than the taxonomy suggests: most peer-reviewed cortical models share a canonical four-population skeleton (PYR/PV/SST/VIP), differ mainly in plasticity rules and layering, and were tuned against overlapping experimental phenomenology ({numref}`fig-sec12-model-architecture-landscape`). Second, the classes of model ({numref}`fig-sec12-model-type-taxonomy`) do not partition cleanly by mechanism — the same behavioral phenomenon (gain modulation, oscillation coherence, prediction-error) is reproduced by minimal rate networks and by Allen-scale biophysical simulations with comparable qualitative success, so model complexity has not been a deciding experiment. Where models diverge decisively is in their *commitments* — to predictive-coding variables, to plasticity rules, to heterogeneous disinhibition — and those commitments are where empirical falsification must be directed.

Throughout, we follow the review's convention of using "SST" in prose and reserving "SOM" only for direct quotations or labels that appeared in original figures. We write VIP→SST→PYR for the canonical disinhibitory arrow convention and specify plasticity rules as "none / short-term (STP) / Hebbian / three-factor / inhibitory" when a model is classified. Because many of the models surveyed come from a narrow set of labs, we name authors and DOIs where single studies drive a claim.

## 12.1 Biophysical single-cell models of VIP firing

Among interneuron classes, biophysical single-cell models of VIP cells are the sparsest. PV-basket fast-spiking and SST Martinotti cells have had multiple generations of Hodgkin–Huxley models tuned to their canonical intrinsic signatures, whereas VIP-targeted single-cell models remain few, and most are embedded as one of several cell types in hippocampal or cortical circuit simulations rather than as standalone reference cells. The best-developed strand fits biophysical models to Patch-seq or whole-cell recordings from identified VIP/CR+ or VIP/CCK+ neurons.

{cite:t}`Meng2023` built conductance-based models that span interneuron-specific classes in CA1, including VIP+ types, and demonstrated that a handful of potassium conductances (notably the D-type current) are sufficient to reproduce the irregular-spiking firing pattern characteristic of VIP/CR+ bipolar cells. {cite:t}`Bernaerts2025` developed a Patch-seq-linked single-neuron modeling framework in which transcriptomic features map onto ion-channel conductances within individual biophysically detailed cells, connecting molecular identity (see {ref}`sec-molecular-identity`) to the firing phenotypes reported in {ref}`sec-electrophysiology`. {cite:t}`GuetMcCreight2016` fit NEURON models to the firing patterns of interneuron-selective type-III cells (IS3) — a VIP+/CR+ hippocampal population — and showed that the morphological compactness and specific K+-channel compositions constrain which input patterns drive reliable IS3 firing. Recent single-cell electrophysiology ({cite:p}`Moreno2026`) identified constitutive HCN (I_h) and Kir currents in layer 1b medial prefrontal cortex VIP interneurons that appear to critically regulate passive properties and EPSP–spike coupling, suggesting that these VIP subtypes may occupy a distinct subthreshold regime relative to L2/3 counterparts.

Two consequences follow. First, existing VIP single-cell models are region- and subtype-specific (hippocampal IS3, cortical L1b, or generic patch-seq-derived cells) and do not constitute a canonical "VIP neuron" model in the sense that PV-basket Hodgkin–Huxley models do. Second, where these biophysical constraints have been fed upward into circuit models, they affect functional predictions: {cite:t}`Cattani2024` coupled biophysical interneuron models — including VIP-like units — into a canonical cortical motif and found that intrinsic heterogeneity changes the dynamic range of the disinhibitory switch. This is a direct instance of the bidirectional cross-reference in the scaffold: intrinsic parameters from {ref}`sec-electrophysiology` are the input to biophysical single-cell models, which then constrain what circuit-level models can plausibly assume.

Two limitations of the current single-cell literature warrant explicit naming. First, no published cortical VIP model is routinely compiled against human patch-clamp data; Patch-seq-driven frameworks ({cite:p}`Bernaerts2025`) are the closest approximation, and cross-species translation of these models to human cortex is not yet validated. Second, the Kcnt1-gain-of-function biophysical/modeling study ({cite:p}`Shore2024`) shows that a single channel mutation produces differential intrinsic effects across cell types, highlighting that predicting cell-type-specific shifts *a priori* remains challenging. Together these gaps mean that multi-scale integration — from transcriptomic identity to behavior — cannot presently be done end-to-end for VIP.

:::{dropdown} Why so few cortical VIP single-cell models?
:icon: info
:color: info

Three field-level reasons recur in the cited literature. (i) Patch-seq–identified VIP populations are internally heterogeneous ({cite:p}`Bernaerts2025,Meng2023`), and electrophysiological and immunohistochemical characterization further suggests substantial within-class diversity ({cite:p}`Moreno2026`), so a single "canonical" parameter set is ambiguous. (ii) The most computationally distinctive VIP property — irregular-spiking — depends on subtly tuned K+ conductances that published datasets under-constrain ({cite:p}`Meng2023,GuetMcCreight2016`). (iii) Circuit modelers have tended to use reduced VIP units (LIF or rate) because the downstream questions — gain, oscillation, prediction error — did not require sub-spike-time biophysics, and there is no documented test showing that detailed VIP biophysics changes macroscopic circuit predictions in a way that overturns canonical conclusions.
:::

## 12.2 Rate, mean-field, and spiking network models with VIP–SST–PV–PYR architecture

Once the VIP cell is abstracted to a rate unit or a leaky integrate-and-fire population, the model space becomes much richer and much more homogeneous. Many cortical VIP-containing network models published since 2018 adopt a similar four-population skeleton — pyramidal, PV, SST, VIP — wiring VIP→SST inhibitorily, SST→dendrite of PYR inhibitorily, and PV→PYR perisomatically inhibitorily, and letting PYR excite all three interneuron classes (reviewed in {cite:p}`Hertag2019,Hahn2022`; see also, e.g., {cite:p}`Wagatsuma2022a,Huang2025,Jiang2024a,Moreni2025a,Wagatsuma2025`). The cell classes named in this recurring skeleton match the three interneuron populations identified in {ref}`sec-synaptic-connectivity` and the canonical motif of {ref}`sec-circuit-motifs`.

**The reduced four-population rate models.** {cite:t}`Hertag2019` (bioRxiv preprint 2018) introduced a rate model in which mutual VIP↔SST antagonism interacts with PV-mediated perisomatic inhibition to redistribute somato-dendritic inhibition in response to top-down input, producing a dendritic gate whose open/closed state tracks the VIP cell's activity, and formalized the result that the inhibitory subnetwork (PV, SST, VIP) can sit in a paradoxical regime in which direct excitation of an inhibitory population *decreases* its firing rate because of the indirect effect on the other two. This paradoxical decomposition is one of the few quantitative predictions of the four-population skeleton that does not depend on VIP specifically, and {cite:t}`Jiang2024a` discusses the paradoxical effect in the context of prior literature on inhibitory-stabilized networks. Where VIP matters in these models is in setting the sign of top-down gain: a VIP impulse reliably disinhibits PYR in the canonical regime, and this is the formal counterpart of the classic VIP-disinhibition motif described in {cite:t}`Pi2013`, {cite:t}`Pfeffer2013`, and {cite:t}`Fu2014`.

**Minimal spiking implementations.** Wagatsuma's group has developed the most sustained microcircuit-spiking line. {cite:t}`Wagatsuma2022a` implemented a four-population LIF network in which PV activation drives gamma oscillations and SST activation drives beta-band modulation during simulated attentional engagement; VIP activation acts as a disinhibitory gate that scales gamma power and rotates the attention axis. {cite:t}`Wagatsuma2022b` showed that the same skeleton can be driven between oscillation and rate regimes by modest changes in VIP/SST balance, and {cite:t}`Wagatsuma2025` extended the framework to flow-direction effects in cortical microcircuits. {cite:t}`Huang2025` used a firing-rate model with feature-selective SOM and VIP interneurons, in which SOM→PC dendritic inhibition and VIP→PV disinhibition account for expectation suppression of sensory responses. {cite:t}`Kim2025b` pushed into a spatial mean-field regime with heterogeneous disinhibition — i.e. VIP connectivity with spatial structure rather than population-average rates — and argued that the spatial structure alone can produce gain patterns that local mean-field models miss.

**Multilayer microcircuit models.** {cite:t}`Hahn2022` built a multilayer V1 rate/spiking model, and {cite:t}`Jiang2024a` built a layered somatosensory-cortex (S1) model with cell-type-specific short-term plasticity (STP); both use published interneuron connectivity data for their respective areas. {cite:t}`Hahn2022` reproduces rate↔oscillation switching and laminar gain; {cite:t}`Jiang2024a` reports that STP modulates responses of excitatory, SOM, and VIP cells to cell-type-specific stimulation by changing dominant inhibitory pathways in their S1 model.

The key empirical phenomena that these rate and spiking networks have been used to explain are: gain modulation of principal-cell firing by locomotion or arousal ({ref}`sec-in-vivo-behavior`); the paradoxical effect on inhibitory populations; arousal- and attention-related oscillatory power changes; novelty and expectation suppression; and top-down gating of dendritic inhibition. Each of these phenomena is reproduced by *at least one* four-population model, but crucially — and this is the first major conflict the section surfaces — no behavioral phenomenon is uniquely reproduced by models that require VIP-driven disinhibition. {cite:t}`Hahn2022` proposes that layered rate-oscillatory switching in V1 is driven by layer-specific recruitment of PV and VIP as co-equal triggers of the disinhibited regime, with SST modulated accordingly, whereas {cite:t}`Dipoppa2018` reports that a VIP-SST disinhibitory model alone is insufficient to explain locomotion effects and instead proposes that modulation of feedforward and recurrent synaptic weights is required ({cite:p}`Dipoppa2018`). This is the model-vs-data gain-mechanism conflict flagged by the evidence package (conflict c12-model-vs-data-gain, {cite:p}`Hahn2022` vs {cite:p}`Dipoppa2018`), and it is unresolved: neither side has published a perturbation experiment of the form "freeze the PV-driven gain term and measure whether locomotor gain persists" that would adjudicate it.

:::{dropdown} What "canonical" actually specifies in these models
:icon: info
:color: info

Across the 18 models tabulated in {numref}`fig-sec12-model-architecture-landscape`, "canonical VIP→SST→PYR" means the *sign* of three connections is fixed: VIP→SST inhibitory, SST→PYR (dendrite) inhibitory, PV→PYR (soma) inhibitory. It does *not* uniquely specify: (i) the sign and strength of VIP→PV connections (some models include a weak VIP→PV inhibition, others omit it); (ii) VIP↔VIP recurrent connectivity; (iii) SST→VIP connectivity, which {cite:t}`Hertag2019` make bidirectional but {cite:t}`Wagatsuma2022a` treat as weak; (iv) PYR→VIP driving strength relative to top-down inputs. These parameters are the main knobs by which models fit different datasets, and cross-study comparison should be read with this in mind.
:::

### 12.2.1 Large-scale biophysical and data-driven models

At the other end of the abstraction scale, data-driven mesocircuit models — Blue Brain–style neocortex simulations and Allen V1/Potjans–Diesmann analogs — include VIP interneurons but treat them as one of several 5HT3a-derived populations rather than as a distinguished functional unit. {cite:t}`Reimann2026` and {cite:t}`Isbister2026` released anatomy- and physiology-constrained mesocircuit simulations in which VIP cells appear at population densities matched to experimental estimates, with connection probabilities drawn from the same datasets used to constrain PV and SST connectivity. These platforms are descriptive — they reproduce spontaneous and evoked statistics — but, with the partial exception of targeted VIP-perturbation simulations, they have not yet been used to derive VIP-specific predictions that differ from those of minimal four-population models. A related neuromorphic implementation ({cite:p}`Iqbal2024,Iqbal2025`) instantiates L2/3 circuit primitives on hardware, often with VIP simplified or abstracted.

This raises the second major conflict. The scaffold explicitly flags c12-minimal-vs-detailed: whether minimal-architecture models outperform or match detailed Allen/mesocircuit-scale models on behavior-linked phenomena. To date, no peer-reviewed study has demonstrated that a detailed Allen V1–scale model captures a gain, oscillation or prediction-error phenomenon that a minimal rate or LIF four-population model fails to reproduce qualitatively. The large-scale models ({cite:p}`Reimann2026,Isbister2026`) buy better anatomical realism and have demonstrated predictive contributions within their validated domains; however, whether they offer broader predictive leverage across a wider range of VIP-related phenomena compared to minimal architectures remains to be established. This is a non-trivial state for the field to occupy — it means that computational-cost arguments and parameter-count arguments may currently favor minimal architectures for some VIP questions, unless and until a mesocircuit simulation produces a falsifiable prediction that a minimal model cannot.

## 12.3 Disinhibition as gain modulation: predictions and tests

The single most-tested computational claim in the VIP literature is that VIP-driven disinhibition implements a multiplicative gain on pyramidal responses. Gain here has a precise meaning: it is the slope of the input–output curve of the pyramidal population, operationally measured as the change in firing-rate response to a fixed sensory drive when a top-down signal engages VIP. The four-population rate models {cite:p}`Hertag2019` and the spiking microcircuit models {cite:p}`Wagatsuma2022a,Moreni2025a` reproduce gain modulation qualitatively; {cite:t}`Huang2025`, using a firing-rate model, reproduces expectation suppression via inhibitory interneurons rather than gain modulation per se, while {cite:t}`Wagatsuma2025` reports E/I-balance-dependent modulation of oscillatory dynamics and information flow rather than gain modulation in the traditional sense. The open questions are (i) whether the gain change is multiplicative or additive, (ii) whether disinhibition is necessary or merely sufficient, and (iii) whether the empirically observed gain correlates with VIP activity in the way that models predict.

The first question has a partial modeling answer. In the canonical rate model, the gain change is approximately multiplicative for small perturbations because the VIP→SST→dendrite chain scales the effective gain of a dendritic non-linearity rather than adding a tonic drive; this is {cite:t}`Hertag2019`'s formal result — a rate-model proof. But the multiplicativity depends on operating in a regime in which the SST→PYR synapse is strong relative to PV→PYR; outside this regime the effect is closer to additive. {cite:t}`Hahn2022` showed that the regime can flip within a single multilayer V1 model as a function of firing rate, so empirical gain measurements at quiescent vs active cortical states may be comparing different operating points rather than different mechanisms.

The second question — necessity vs sufficiency — is where empirical work has created the sharpest tension. {cite:t}`MyersJoseph2022` reported that, in mouse V1, attentional modulation is *orthogonal* to the disinhibition axis produced by VIP activation; models that assume attentional gain must flow through VIP→SST→PYR therefore miss the dominant variance. {cite:t}`Dipoppa2018` had earlier shown that VIP activity rises with arousal and covaries with gain in V1, but the correlation is not a dissociation test. In auditory cortex, by contrast, {cite:t}`Yavorska2021` report that locomotor effects are not mediated by the VIP network, indicating that VIP-driven locomotor gain is not a universal cortical mechanism and is contested across sensory areas. Taken together, disinhibition is *sufficient* to produce a gain change in models, and it is *observed to covary* with gain in awake V1, but the {cite:p}`MyersJoseph2022` result warns that the dominant axis of gain modulation may be PV-driven or input-driven rather than VIP-driven in some tasks. {cite:t}`Szadai2022` further complicates the picture by showing cortex-wide, reward/punishment-structured VIP responses that do not track a canonical attention axis.

The third question — does the empirically observed gain actually correlate with VIP activity? — has received region-specific answers. {cite:t}`Lenkey2025` (preprint {cite:p}`Vervaeke2024`) reported robust VIP-mediated gain modulation of place cells in neocortical retrosplenial cortex (RSC) but minimal impact in hippocampal CA1, suggesting that VIP output scales place-cell responses in a region-specific manner. Cortical equivalents are softer: {cite:t}`Dipoppa2018` report that locomotion modulates VIP responses in complex, stimulus-dependent ways in V1, and other studies suggest heterogeneous effects across tasks ({cite:p}`Szadai2022,MyersJoseph2022`). This regional heterogeneity means that a single cortical model is unlikely to be universally predictive, and motivates the spatial-mean-field approach of {cite:t}`Kim2025b`.

A model-mechanism dissociation worth naming explicitly: in {cite:t}`Rademacher2025`'s rate model of ketamine-induced gamma changes, the PV and SST interneurons together account for the observed MEG gamma-band shifts without requiring VIP-specific gain changes. The model does not deny that VIP matters; it shows that the particular experimental signature — ketamine-induced gamma increase — is explicable with the PV/SST arm of the canonical circuit alone. The methodological lesson is that matching a single empirical signature is a weak test of whether a model's VIP component is doing causal work.

## 12.4 Predictive-coding, top-down, and reinforcement-learning models with VIP

The predictive-coding family is where the VIP-circuit modeling literature has made its boldest commitments. The canonical claim is that negative and positive prediction-error neurons in layer 2/3 are implemented by specific interneuron motifs — and the open dispute is *which* motif. {cite:t}`Hertag2020` proposed that a canonical interneuron motif (PYR, PV, SST, VIP) with dendritic compartmentalization and a three-factor plasticity rule produces prediction-error neurons — both negative-error (firing when input is less than predicted) and positive-error (firing when input exceeds prediction) — in layer 2/3. VIP plays a distinctive role: it disinhibits the dendritic compartment of PYR cells in response to top-down prediction signals, so that the mismatch between top-down and bottom-up drive is read out at the PYR soma as a prediction error.

{cite:t}`Waitzmann2024` (and its preprint antecedent {cite:p}`Waitzmann2023`) took a different approach. Their model proposes that short-term-plasticity-gated microcircuit motifs with different cell-type-specific wiring can account for the reversal of SST interneuron responses under different visual stimulation conditions during locomotion-induced top-down modulation, without requiring VIP-driven disinhibition. This is the architectural-assumptions conflict surfaced by the evidence package ({cite:p}`Hertag2020` vs {cite:p}`Waitzmann2024`, conflict status: unresolved): both models propose cell-type-specific microcircuit mechanisms for context-dependent cortical modulation, but they differ in which cell-type-specific motif is doing the work and in the phenomenology they address — mismatch responses in the case of {cite:t}`Hertag2020` versus locomotion-induced gain modulation in the case of {cite:t}`Waitzmann2024`.

{cite:t}`Nemati2025` adds a fresh data point by showing that a canonical spiking circuit augmented with explicit prediction-error variables reproduces expectation/mismatch signals in V1 without requiring a novel learning rule; {cite:t}`Lee2025` demonstrates that multi-interneuron cortical networks generate *oscillatory* patterns during predictive coding that depend on VIP recruitment. {cite:t}`Rossbroich2025` and {cite:t}`Sharafeldin2025` push toward task-level formalization: Sharafeldin's framework assigns algorithmic roles to interneuron subtypes within an integrated predictive coding and reinforcement learning model, with the VIP-SST disinhibitory motif enabling context-dependent gating of excitatory activity and balancing energy efficiency with representational capacity.

The practical upshot is that four-plus model architectures — {cite:p}`Hertag2020,Waitzmann2024,Nemati2025,Sharafeldin2025,Rossbroich2025,Lee2025` — each address overlapping subsets of cortical mismatch and adaptation phenomenology, though some focus on predictive coding specifically (e.g., {cite:p}`Hertag2020,Nemati2025,Rossbroich2025,Lee2025`), others on response reversal and top-down modulation ({cite:p}`Waitzmann2024`), and still others on normative comparisons between predictive-coding and mechanistic baselines ({cite:p}`Sharafeldin2025`). No published experiment clearly adjudicates among them. A decisive test would require either (i) a cell-type-specific perturbation during a mismatch task that dissociates VIP-driven disinhibition from STP-gated alternatives ({cite:p}`Keller2020a,Waitzmann2024`), or (ii) a biophysical measurement of the timescale of the error signal that distinguishes three-factor from STP substrates. Neither is on the immediate horizon.

**Reinforcement learning and attention.** A smaller but growing set of models treats VIP as the interneuron class that conveys reward and prediction signals from neuromodulatory systems into cortical microcircuits. A modeling line argues that VIP interneurons can implement credit assignment for cortical reinforcement learning: {cite:t}`Chevy2024` proposes a cortical-microcircuit model for region-specific credit assignment in RL. {cite:t}`Shen2023` contributes the canonical VIP→SST disinhibition motif that these models rely on. The empirical grounding for VIP-carried reward signals rests on a smaller and mostly preprint literature. {cite:t}`Sharafeldin2025` generalizes this by showing that a belief-update rule with cell-type-specific reward/prediction channels can use VIP as the top-down/neuromodulatory gate while keeping PV and SST in their classical gain/dendritic-inhibition roles. These RL-flavored models are closer to functional claims (what VIP *is for*) than to mechanistic ones (how VIP actually drives the circuit), and they inherit both the strengths and the limitations of the rate/spiking microcircuits they are built on. In particular, the RL-attention literature has not yet produced a VIP-specific falsification test; it has produced attractive formal analogs of behavioral phenomena.

The cross-reference back to {ref}`sec-in-vivo-behavior` is important: models in the RL family are typically validated against a narrow set of in-vivo experiments (mouse V1 attentional tasks, mPFC reward tasks), and those experiments inherit the regional and subtype heterogeneity problems that {ref}`sec-in-vivo-behavior` catalogs.

## 12.5 Plasticity-centered VIP-circuit models

Plasticity is where model architectures diverge most sharply in their commitments. Static-weight models treat the VIP→SST→PYR arrow as a hardwired routing scheme; plasticity-centered models ask which synapses must be modifiable and under what rule for the observed circuit behavior to emerge. {cite:t}`Schulz2021` introduced an inhibitory-plasticity model in which novelty responses emerge from selective strengthening of inhibitory-to-excitatory synapses via a spike-timing-dependent rule that implements negative feedback, with disinhibition proposed to regulate the amplification of novelty responses. {cite:t}`Jiang2024a` paired a layered architecture with cell-type-specific short-term plasticity; in their model, STP modulates cell-type-specific stimulation responses and can reproduce sensory responses of specific interneuron types. {cite:t}`Moreni2025b` augmented the layered column with STDP and inhibitory plasticity together and found that VIP-containing circuits sustain oscillations across a wider operating regime than non-plastic controls. {cite:t}`Jabonska2025a` addressed interneuron-specific synaptic plasticity rules that modify the canonical motif.

Two substantive conflicts emerge in this subfield. First, {cite:t}`Favila2025` — working in amygdala rather than cortex — reports *heterogeneous* plasticity across interneuron subtypes during associative learning, with VIP showing a distinctive pattern relative to PV and SST. The audit excluded this paper from the cortical taxonomy figure for scope reasons, but its cross-species/cross-region message is relevant: cortical plasticity models that assume homogeneous rules across VIP populations may be under-specifying a heterogeneity that exists in at least one limbic analog. Second, several cortical plasticity schemes — inhibitory plasticity ({cite:p}`Schulz2021`), STP-modulated network dynamics ({cite:p}`Jiang2024a`) and STDP/STP ({cite:p}`Moreni2025b`), and three-factor or prediction-error-modulated Hebbian learning ({cite:p}`Hertag2020,Sharafeldin2025,Lee2025`) — each propose to account for a subset of learning-phenotype data, and no published study has done the pairwise falsification experiment (matched stimulus protocol, matched circuit readout, cell-type-specific perturbation) to adjudicate among them.

## 12.6 Oscillations: VIP in gamma- and beta-band regimes

Oscillatory phenomenology is a primary target of spiking network models with VIP ({cite:p}`Wagatsuma2022a,Hahn2022,Rademacher2025,Moreni2025b,Veit2023`) and firing-rate network models ({cite:p}`Lee2025`). The bidirectional cross-reference to {ref}`sec-oscillations` makes this a natural place to compare two recent contributions — {cite:t}`Veit2023` and {cite:t}`Lee2025` — whose oscillation-level framings differ in sign and scope, though the disagreement is not supported by the evidence package as a full model–experiment conflict on par with the three enumerated in §12.8.

{cite:t}`Veit2023` reports that cortical VIP neurons *locally control the gain* of gamma-band rhythms but *globally control the coherence* — that is, VIP perturbation produces a local gain change and a spatially dissociable coherence effect with an observable sign and area-scaling. {cite:t}`Lee2025` (under the review's filtered citation key) models multi-interneuron cortical networks generating oscillatory patterns during predictive coding and finds that VIP recruitment shapes the *oscillatory regime* — the balance between gamma and slower rhythms — with a directional sign at least partly opposite to that reported in Veit's framework for some stimulus conditions. This difference in framing is unresolved and plausibly reflects different experimental protocols (acute optogenetic perturbation vs task-engaged behavior), different definitions of coherence (global PPC vs local LFP coupling), and different network regimes. A clean model–data resolution would require a shared stimulus protocol in which both groups' analysis pipelines were applied to the same cortical recordings.

{cite:t}`ReynerParra2022` proposes a formal result: in a minimal E/I network, communication effectiveness depends on whether the input frequency is higher than the natural gamma cycle, which is shaped by the interplay of excitatory drive and inhibitory synaptic timing. This suggests that an apparently direction-disagreeing result between two labs may reflect operating in different parts of a frequency-matching manifold. Together, {cite:t}`ReynerParra2022` and {cite:t}`Sengupta2025` may be read as suggesting that oscillation direction depends on *circuit regime* rather than being solely a *cell-type property*, though neither paper frames its conclusions in exactly those terms.

## 12.7 Assumptions, limitations, and what models actually explain

Five assumptions recur across nearly all of the models tabulated in {numref}`fig-sec12-model-architecture-landscape`, and it is worth naming them because they bound the explanatory reach of the field.

1. **Homogeneous VIP population.** Rate and spiking microcircuit models treat VIP as a single functional population. The transcriptomic and developmental heterogeneity adjudicated in {ref}`sec-molecular-identity` and {ref}`sec-development` — VIP/CR+ bipolar vs VIP/CCK+ basket vs VIP/ChAT — is effectively collapsed. Recent models that introduce spatial heterogeneity ({cite:p}`Kim2025b`) or feature-selective projections ({cite:p}`Huang2025`) are a first step away from this assumption, but no published cortical circuit model simulates the three VIP subtypes as distinct cell classes with their documented intrinsic and synaptic differences.
2. **Canonical wiring sign convention.** The arrows VIP→SST inhibitory, SST→dendrite inhibitory, PV→PYR perisomatic inhibitory are fixed in almost every cortical model. The cross-subtype variability in connectivity noted in {ref}`sec-synaptic-connectivity` and the region-specific dissociations of the canonical motif in {ref}`sec-circuit-motifs` are therefore not represented. Models that explicitly depart from this convention — spatially heterogeneous mean-field ({cite:p}`Kim2025b`), alternative STP-based mechanisms for nonlinear response phenomena ({cite:p}`Waitzmann2024`), mutual VIP↔SST balance ({cite:p}`Hertag2019`) — remain in the minority.
3. **Stationary baselines for perturbation predictions.** Most models predict the effect of "VIP activation" or "VIP silencing" as a steady-state shift. Behavioral experiments operate in transient regimes where states (arousal, locomotion, task engagement) change on second-to-minute timescales. {cite:t}`Hahn2022` showed that the same circuit can occupy different regimes as a function of state, so single-state predictions from stationary models will fail to match in-vivo time-varying data except in the limit.
4. **Single-region focus.** Almost all cortical VIP models are single-region or single-column. VIP activity tracks cortex-wide reward/punishment structure ({cite:p}`Szadai2022`) and shows region-specific gain modulation, robust in RSC but minimal in hippocampus ({cite:p}`Lenkey2025`). Multi-area models that treat VIP as a bridge between top-down and bottom-up streams are essentially absent from the peer-reviewed literature.
5. **Abstracted plasticity.** Even plasticity-centered models use a single rule across all synapses of a given class. The experimental record in cortex ({ref}`sec-development`) and amygdala ({cite:p}`Favila2025`) suggests this is wrong in at least some contexts. Models have not yet reconciled heterogeneous plasticity with homogeneous population dynamics.

Against this list of limitations, the accounting of what models *do* explain is concrete. Canonical four-population rate and spiking models explain: (i) the basic phenomenology of VIP-mediated disinhibition observed in slice and in vivo ({cite:p}`Pi2013,Keller2020a,Kuchibhotla2016`); (ii) arousal-, locomotion-, and attention-linked gain of pyramidal responses in V1, though specific mechanisms differ—{cite:t}`Dipoppa2018` model locomotor gain via feedforward/recurrent synaptic reweighting rather than purely VIP-mediated disinhibition, while {cite:t}`Wagatsuma2022a` model attention-linked rather than arousal/locomotion-linked modulation ({cite:p}`Dipoppa2018,Hertag2019,Wagatsuma2022a`); (iii) gamma-band and beta-band modulation of cortical oscillations by PV/SST with VIP as a modulator ({cite:p}`Wagatsuma2022a,Hahn2022,Rademacher2025`); (iv) novelty, mismatch, and expectation-suppression responses under appropriate plasticity rules—{cite:t}`Huang2025` specifically model expectation suppression rather than novelty/mismatch signals ({cite:p}`Schulz2021,Hertag2020,Huang2025`); (v) place-cell gain modulation via the VIP→SST arm, though {cite:t}`Lenkey2025` report that this disinhibition strongly modulates RSC-like circuits while having minimal effect in hippocampal CA1 due to dendritic compartmentalization ({cite:p}`Lenkey2025,Meng2023,Bilash2023`). What they do *not* yet explain: VIP subtype-specific behavioral contributions; the sign of gamma coherence disagreement between Veit and Lee 2025 regimes; the cross-species scaling of VIP heterogeneity into human cortex ({ref}`sec-species-translation`).

## 12.8 Model–experiment correspondence and falsification

What would decisive adjudication look like? For each of the three conflicts surfaced by the evidence package, a falsification test can be specified in terms of measurable quantities, but none of those tests is yet operational in the literature.

**Conflict 1 — predictive-coding architecture and response-reversal mechanisms ({cite:p}`Hertag2020` vs {cite:p}`Waitzmann2024`).** The substrate of error computation in predictive-coding models is unresolved: {cite:t}`Hertag2020` proposes canonical three-factor VIP-driven disinhibition, whereas {cite:t}`Waitzmann2024` proposes STP-gated mechanisms that can produce response reversal in SST neurons, which may bear on mismatch signals but does not directly address predictive-coding error computation. Both models fit extant mismatch data. A decisive test is a matched mismatch paradigm with a cell-type-specific VIP silencing arm; under Hertag2020 the negative-error response should be abolished, under Waitzmann2024's STP-gated account it may persist. This test has not been published for mouse V1 with the required controls.

**Conflict 2 — model-vs-data gain mechanism ({cite:p}`Hahn2022` vs {cite:p}`Dipoppa2018`).** In Hahn2022's multilayer rate-spiking model the dominant gain mechanism is PV-driven in high-firing regimes, whereas {cite:t}`Dipoppa2018`'s locomotor-gain model of V1 proposes that VIP-mediated disinhibition alone is insufficient and that gain requires modulation of feedforward and recurrent synapses. The conflict is flagged as "partial" in the evidence package because Hahn2022 does not directly deny VIP involvement; it asserts that PV dynamics dominate within a specific operating regime. Adjudication requires state-matched experiments — simultaneous measurement of VIP, PV, and SST activity during graded locomotion with layer-resolved firing-rate readouts — which recent cortex-wide imaging studies ({cite:p}`Szadai2022`) approach but have not yet completed.

**Conflict 3 — minimal vs detailed architecture ({cite:p}`Jiang2024a`).** {cite:t}`Jiang2024a` demonstrates that a three-interneuron architecture with cell-type-specific short-term plasticity modulates network responses and reproduces sensory response features in S1, raising the question of when such architectural detail is needed versus when simpler models suffice. The conflict has not been experimentally framed because no empirical signature has yet been proposed that distinguishes minimal from detailed model predictions in a region-matched comparison. Until such a signature is named, adjudication remains open for laminar S1 phenomena. The large-scale biophysical platforms of {cite:t}`Reimann2026` and {cite:t}`Isbister2026` sit on the detailed side of this axis; while both report specific predictions — for example, regarding connectivity motifs, spatial targeting by inhibitory subpopulations, and interneuron roles in stimulus encoding — it remains an open question, in our assessment, whether these predictions uniquely require detailed architectures that minimal models cannot match.

A fifth, less obvious conflict bears brief mention: the {cite:t}`Preuss2025` finding of inhibitory circuit motifs in cortical somatosensory L5 SST interneurons reorganizes the downstream geometry of the canonical model, because L5 SST→PYR is no longer a simple dendritic inhibition arrow but a multi-motif circuit. Cortical models that collapse L5 SST into the canonical skeleton ({cite:p}`Wagatsuma2022a,Hahn2022`)—and those that, like {cite:t}`Jiang2024a`, use uniform electrophysiological parameters for L5 and L6 SOM cells based on available data—may therefore be under-specifying the output stage in deep layers. This is not yet a published model-vs-data disagreement, but it is a latent one.

{cite:t}`Sengupta2025` build on prior work showing that a large-enough network of pyramidal cells with interneuron feedback can initiate theta rhythms consistent with hippocampal CA1 data, and propose a mean-field model in which pyramidal and bistratified cells govern theta rhythm generation without requiring ad hoc oscillator assumptions. This kind of mechanistic-parsimony test — show that an established circuit generates the phenomenology on its own — is the inverse of the VIP-specific tests above, and it remains rare in the cortical VIP modeling literature.

## 12.9 Looking ahead

The computational-models class occupies an uncomfortable position in the review's argument arc. It fits Act 3 because model–data correspondence is how the field decides what a heterogeneous cell class actually *does*, but the current state of the literature is that models can be made to fit almost any single experimental phenomenon about VIP, and the classes of model (rate, spiking, laminar, predictive-coding, RL, plasticity) do not partition cleanly by mechanism. Two shifts would break the deadlock. First, models that *commit* to VIP subtype heterogeneity — bipolar vs basket vs cholinergic VIP — as separate dynamical populations, because this is where the empirical data points, and because such commitment would generate testable within-class dissociations. Second, pre-registered cross-lab perturbation experiments aimed at the three conflicts enumerated above, with shared analysis pipelines. Neither shift requires new modeling mathematics; both require changes to how modelers and experimentalists jointly operate.

The synthesis in {ref}`sec-synthesis` returns to this point: the computational-models class contributes the least-contested evidence in the review on *sufficiency* (VIP-containing canonical architectures can reproduce virtually any VIP-related phenomenon) and the most-contested evidence on *necessity*. The translational grade follows directly — models have proved indispensable for interpreting perturbation experiments but have not yet earned the ability to deliver cell-type-specific therapeutic predictions in the sense that detailed PV models have for seizure and schizophrenia phenotypes ({cite:p}`Rademacher2025`).
